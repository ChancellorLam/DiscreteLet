Logic Introduction:

Logic is the study of valid reasoning, argumentation, and deduction. Key areas of study include the fundamental laws of logic (like identity and non-contradiction), meaning of symbols, structure of arguments, and the relationship between logic and truth. Logic is a foundational element to philosophy, mathematics as well as computer science.

1. Logical Symbols

Propositional logic is considered to be the most basic form of logic. It serves as a foundational system that studies the logical relationships of propositions/statements, which are treated as units that are either true or false. It forms the basis of more complex logical systems like first-order-logic. Propositional logic deals with the truth values of propositions and how they combine using logical connectives. In the context of propositional logic, there are five main logical operators:

  • - NOT (Negation)
    • Example: Let Then
  • - AND (Conjunction)
    • Example: Let and Then
  • - OR (Disjunction)
    • Example: Let and Then
    • Note that in everyday English, OR is commonly used to mean one or the other. For example, consider the sentence "You are good at math or you are good at English." This is normally interpreted to mean that you are only good at one and not good at both. (This is actually a more complex logical operator called XOR/exclusive OR). In the context of logic however, OR means if at least one of the propositions are true, then the whole statement is true.

2. GCD & LCM

The greatest common divisor (GCD) of two numbers is the largest integer that divides both, while the least common multiple (LCM) is the smallest positive integer that both numbers divide into evenly. One way to find these is through prime factorization, by comparing shared and unique factors. Another is the Euclidean Algorithm, which uses repeated division to efficiently determine the GCD. These concepts are essential for simplifying fractions, solving equations, and understanding number relationships.
